Anxiety Disorders

Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

Generalized Anxiety Disorder, or GAD, is mostly genetic and very common, with more than three million cases in the United States per year. A person with GAD feels extreme and constant anxiety about parts of their life they cannot control. This interferes with a person's day-to-day life and people with GAD may have difficulty concentrating and relaxing. Generalized Anxiety Disorder may also cause an increased risk of heart disease. Some symptoms include insomnia, headaches, and Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS), which is a combination of diahrrea and pain. GAD is most commonly found in teenagers aged eighteen to nineteen years old. Women are also twice as likely as men to have GAD. Generalized Anxiety Disorder is treatable, with two treatments being psychotherapy and anti-depressant medications.

Panic Disorder

Panic Disorder is characterized by unexpected and recurring panic attacks, or episodes of intense fear. The panic attacks are often triggered by a feared object or situation and a person experiencing it feels out of control as well as a sense of impending doom. They usually fear of having another panic attack. Some symptoms include a fast heartbeat, dizziness, shaking, trouble breathing, and chest pain. Panic Disorder is most commonly found in demales aged thirty to fourty-four years old. Panic Disorder is treatable, generally with psychotherapy and anti-anxiety medications.

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, or PTSD, is very common, occuring in more than three million cases in the United States per year, and may be developed after experiencing a tramautic or dangerous event. A person with PTSD may feel stressed or frightened, even if they are not in danger. Some symptoms include recurring memories, avoidance, and being emotionally numb. A person with Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder may also undergo hyperarousal, which involves having difficulty sleeping and being always on edge. PSTD is most commonly found in war veterans, children, and people who have been through assault, abuse, accidents, and other disasters. Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder is treatable, some treatments include Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing (EMDR), and medications.

Social Anxiety Disorder

Social Anxiety Disorder is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year. A person with Social Anxiety Disorder feels intensely anxious about social situations in which others might judge them. They often worry about being embarrassed or rejected and avoid social situations to the point it interferes with their daily life. Some symptoms include sweating, trembling, a rapid heart rate, a rigid body posture, and a sick feeling in the stomach. Social Anxiety Disorder is most common in females aged eighteen to twenty-nine years old. Social Anxiety Disorder is treatable, with Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), psychotherapy, and anti-depressant or sedative medications.

Social Media Anxiety Disorder

Social Media Anxiety Disorder is characterized by constantly checking what friends or followers are doing on social media to not miss out. A person with Social Media Anxiety Disorder often feels extremely anxious if they cannot check their social media accounts for a period of time. Some symptoms include loss of interest in other activities, withdrawal from friends and family, and being distracted constantly by social media. Social Media Anxiety Disorder is most common among high users of social media, people with anxiety, and teenagers. Social Media Anxiety Disorder is treatable, mainly by limiting exposure to social media intake in a structured way.

Phobias

Phobias are strong, unrealistic fears of an object or situation that does not really post much, if any, danger. People with phobias avoid objects or situations that they fear. Some symptoms include shortness of breath, a fast heartbeat, panic, and a desire to flee. Common phobias include: Arachnophonia, fear of spiders, Acrophobia, fear of heights, Agoraphobia, fear of open or crowded spaces, Astraphobia, fear of thunder or lightning, Claustraphobia, fear of small spaces, Cynophobia, fear of dogs, Ophidiophobia, fear of snakes, and Trypanophobia, fear of needles. Phobias are treatable, generally with Exposure Therapy and Cognitive Behavior Therapy (CBT).



Eating Disorders

Anorexia

Anorexia, also known as Anorexia Nervosa, is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year. People with Anorexia obsess about their weight and how they eat. They often have a distorted body image and have an unwarranted fear of being overweight. Some symptoms include trying to maintain an abnormally low body weight through starvation or overexercising. Anorexia is most common in women aged twelve to twenty-five. Anorexia is treatable, usually through support groups, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), and medications.

Bulimia

Bulimia, also known as Bulimia Nervosa, is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year. People with Bulimia binge-eat and then take steps to avoid weight gain. They often use vomiting (purging), excessive exercise, or fasting to lose weight. Some symptoms include living in fear of gaining weight, being preoccupied with body shape, and feeling a loss of control during binge-eating. Bulimia is most common in women aged twelve to twenty-five. Bulimia is treatable, mainly through support groups, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), and medications.

Binge-Eating Disorder

Binge-Eating Disorder is characterized by consuming unusually large amounts of food and feeling unable to stop eating. People with Binge-Eating Disorder feel out of control when binge-eating and binge-eating becoming a regular occurrence. Some symptoms include eating when you are full or not hungry, eating rapidly during binge episodes, and feeling ashamed about eating. Binge-Eating Disorder is most common in younger and middle-aged people, as well as people with Type 1 and Type 2 Diabetes. Binge-Eating Disorder is treatable, by Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), anticonvulsants, and anti-depressants.



Mood Disorders

Bipolar Disorder (Manic Depression)

Bipolar Disorder, also known as Manic Depression, is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year. A person with Bipolar Disorder experiences episodes of mood swings ranging from depressive lows to manic highs. This causes unusual shifts in mood, energy, activity levels, and concentration. Some symptoms include feeling periods of unusually intense emotion, changes in sleep patters, and uncharacteristic behaviors. Bipolar Disorder is most common in women, appearing in the teen or early adult years. Bipolar Disorder cannot be cured, but treatment can help, including Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), anticonvulsants, and antipsychotics.

Major Depressive Disorder (Clinical Depression)

Major Depressive Disorder, also known as Clinical Depression, is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year. A person with Major Depressive Disorder experiences ongoing, intense feelings of sadness, worthlessness, and hopelessness, disrupting their ability to do daily life tasks. They are also more likely to engage in behaviors harmful to their health and are at a greater risk of developing other health conditions. Some symptoms include continuous low mood, having low self-esteem, and having no mtoivation or interest in activities. Clinical Depression is most common in females aged eighteen to twenty-five years old. Major Depressive Disorder is treatable, with the main treatments being Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), psychotherapy, and anti-depressant medications.

Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Persistent Depressive Disorder, also known as Dysthymia, is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year, and is a mild but long-term form of depression. It is characterized by a low mood occurring for at least two years, with at least two other symptoms of depression. Some symptoms include loss of interest in daily activities, hopelessness, low self-esteem, and decreased activity. Persistent Depressive Disorder is most common in females and the average age of onset is thirty-one years old. Dysthymia is treatable, mainly through physical exercise, psychotherapy, and anti-depressants.

Postpartum Depression

Postpartum Depression is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year, and is depression that occurs after childbirth. Some symptoms include insomnia, loss of appetite, intense irritability, and difficulty bonding with the baby. Postpartum Depression is most common in first-time mothers, mothers younger than twenty-five years old, and mothers of twins. Postpartum Depression is treatable, primarily through Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), psychotherapy, and relaxation techniques.

Psychotic Depression

Psychotic Depression is characterized when a severe depressive illness includes some form of psychosis, which includes hallucinations and delusions. Some symptoms include agitation, anxiety, insomnia, intellectual impairment, and physical immobility. Psychotic Depression is most commmon in people admitted to the hospital for depression. Psychotic Depression is treatable and is usually done in a hospital setting, where the patient is given anti-depressant and antipsychotic medications.

Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)

Seasonal Affective Disorder, or SAD, is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year, and causes similar symptoms to those of Major Depressive Disorder. A person with SAD often has such symptoms during the winter months when there is no sunlight. Some symptoms include feelings of worthlessness, extreme tiredness, loss of interest, and irritability. Seasonal Affective Disorder is most common in younger adults, and occurs more often in women than in men. SAD is treatable, mainly by going outside or through light therapy (phototherapy) and anti-depressant medications.



Personality/Behavioral Disorders

Antisocial Personality Disorder

Antisocial Personality Disorder is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year. A person with Antisocial Personality Disorder is often impulsive, irresponsible, and manipulative. Some symptoms include a disregard for social rules, tendency for aggressive behavior, and indifference to other people's rights and feelings. Antisocial Personality Disorder is most common in men, with the disorder usually being developed by the age of eleven. Antisocial Personality Disorder cannot be cured, but treatment can help, which includes family therapy, behavior therapy, and psychotherapy.

Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD)

Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, or ADHD, is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year. It is characterized by difficulty paying attention, hyperactivity, and impulsiveness. Some symptoms include difficulty focusing, organizing, or following instructions, boredom with tasks or activities, and forgetfulness. Attention-Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder is most common in males, and is often first identified in school-aged children. ADHD cannot be cured, but treatment can help, including Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and stimulants.

Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)

Borderline Personality Disorder, or BPD, is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year. It is characterized by unstable moods, behavior, and relationships, severely impacting a person's ability to regulate their emotions. Some symptoms include instability, feelings of worthlessness, insecurity, and impulsivity. Borderline Personality Disorder is most common in women and symptoms are usually present by late adolescence. BPD is treatable, mainly through therapies such as Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Anger Management, and Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT).

Conduct Disorder

Conduct Disorder is characterized by a disregard for others, basic social standards, and rules. A person with Conduct Disorder show hostile and sometimes violent behavior towards other people. Some symptoms include not caring about social norms, engaging in cruel behavior, and committing crimes. Conduct Disorder is most common in males, and occurrs in children and teens. It can also grow into Antisocial Personality Disorder in adulthood. Conduct Disorder is treatable, primarily through Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Family Therapy, and Peer Group Therapy.

Narcissistic Personality Disorder

Narcissistic Personality Disorder is rare, with fewer than 200,000 cases in the United States per year, and is characterized by an exaggerated sense of their own importance. People with Narcissistic Personality Disorder have a deep need for excessive attention and admiration and often lack empathy for others. Some symptoms include a sense of entitlement, fantasies about seccess and power, being envious of others, and taking advantage of others to get what they want. Narcissistic Personality Disorder is most common in males, usually beginning in their teens or early adulthood. Narcissistic Personality Disorder cannot be cured, but treatment can help, through supportive care such as monitoring and psychotherapy.

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder, or OCD, is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year. OCD is characterized by excessive thoughts, obsessions, that lead to repetitive behaviors, compulsions. Some symptoms include fear of contamination or dirt, needing things to be orderly and symmetrical, and intrusive thoughts. Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder is most common in older teens or young adults. It is more common among males in childhood, but among females in adolescence and adulthood. OCD cannot be cured, but treatment can help, mainly with Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Aversion Therapy, and anti-depressant medication.

Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD)

Oppositional Defiant Disorder, or ODD, is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year. People with ODD show behaviors that are uncooperative, defiant, and hostile to other people. These behaviors are also more common when they are hungry, tired, or upset. Some symptoms include frequent temper tantrums, refusal to obey authority figures, and questioning and arguing. They are also easily annoyed by others, speak harshly, and show a great amount of anger. Oppositional Defiant Disorder is most common in male children around eight years old. ODD cannot be cured, but treatment can help, by Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) and Family Therapy.

Paranoid Personality Disorder

Paranoid Personality Disorder is characterized by showing extreme paranoia, or deep mistrust and suspicion, of others without any reason. People with Paranoid Personality Disorder are always on guard and believe that others are constantly trying to hurt or threaten them. Some symptoms include doubting the loyalty of others, having trouble working with others, being hypersensitive to criticism, and quickly becoming hostile. Paranoid Personality Disorder is most common in men, showing signs by their late teens or early adult years. Paranoid Personality Disorder cannot be cured, but treatment can help, largely through Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) or Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT).

Schizoid Personality Disorder

Schizoid Personality Disorder is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year. People with Schizoid Personality Disorder avoid social activities and consistently shy away from interacting with others. Some symptoms include having difficulty relating to others, showing a limited range of emotional expression, and not desiring to enjoy close relationships, even with family members. Schizoid Personality Disorder is most common in men and usually begins by early adulthood. Schizoid Personality Disorder cannot be cured, but treatment can help, with Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) or psychotherapy.

Schizophrenia

Schizophrenia is common, with more than 200,000 cases in the United States per year, and is characterized by irregular thoughts, delusions, or false beliefs and perceptions, and hallucinations. Some symptoms include paranoia, appearing agitated, talking to themselves, and having difficulty managing personal hygiene tasks. Schizophrenia is most common in men aged in their late teens to early thirties. Schizophrenia cannot be cured, but treatment can help, mainly through antipsychotic and anti-tremor medications, as well as psychotherapy.



Substance Use/Addictive Disorders

Alcohol Use Disorder

Alcohol Use Disorder, also known as alcoholism or alcohol dependence, is very common, with more than 3 million cases in the United States per year. Alcoholism is characterized by uncontrolled drinking and proccupation with alcohol. Some symptoms include a strong need or urge to drink alcohol and being unable to limit the amount of alcohol consumed. Alcohol Use Disorder is most common in men, ages twelve to seventeen years old. Alcohol dependence cannot be cured, but treatment can help, including dextoxing, Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), alcoholism medications, and abstinence.

Drug Use Disorder

Drug Use Disorder is characterized by an inability to control usage of legal or illegal drugs or medication. A person with Drug Use Disorder may continue using the drug despite the harm it causes. Some symptoms include having intense urges for the drug that blocks out any other thoughts, spending money on the drug even though they cannot afford it, and experiencing withdrawal symptoms when they attempt to stop taking the drug. Drug Use Disorder is most common in men who are over twelve years old. Drug Use Disorder cannot be cured, but treatment can help, mainly by behavioral counseling, medications, and medical devices and applications used to treat withdrawal symptoms.
